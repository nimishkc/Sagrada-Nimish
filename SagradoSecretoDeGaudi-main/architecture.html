The Crypt (1882 - 1889)

Underneath the Basílica and extending 10 metres below the ground, lies the cathedral’s very own Crypt. It is the very first development of the project, even preceding the years that Antoni Gaudí had joined for its construction.

When Gaudí took over, he made multiple adjustments, yet stayed true to the original design.

It is now known as Antoni Gaudí's place of rest, having been buried there upon his death in 1926.

Read more about the Crypt here.

The Passion Façade (1954 - 1976)

In the western direction, lies the stark and extraordinary Passion façade.

Representing both Jesus’ death and resurrection, this angular façade has been said to resemble a skeleton, in direct contrast to the Nativity façade’s lively organic design.

The two representations have been divided between a lower and upper narthex and contain scenes from Jesus’ final days of life. The scenes can be read like a story in an “s” shaped curve, beginning at the lower left and spanning all the way through the roof of the structure.

When viewing the façade from left to right, you can observe the 4 bell towers dedicated to James the lesser, Bartholomew, Thomas, then Philip.

Read more about the Passion façade here.

The Glory Façade (2002 - today)

The final façade, known as the Glory façade, is found facing the south. It is the only one of which still under construction and will act as the main entrance to the Basílica upon its completion.

It will represent Jesus’ “eternal glory” and all of humanity, with themes of heaven and hell, temptation, and of an ultimate “ascension to God”, based on the design ideas left behind by Antoni Gaudí himself.

As the tallest of all the façades, it will contain one “great narthex” of 16 lanterns, with the bottom 7 representing the days of creation.

The 4 bell towers planned for this façade will represent the 4 remaining apostles: Andrew, Peter, Paul, and James the Greater (in order from left to right).

Read more about the Glory façade here.

Virgin Mary Central Tower (2016 - 2021)

Located at the centre north of the Basílica, this central tower has been specially dedicated to the Virgin Mary.

It is the second tallest tower of the cathedral, at a height of 138 metres, and can be identified by its magnificent morning star embellishment–one that can be seen shining luminously at night.

Read more about the Virgin Mary tower here.

Jesus Christ Central Tower (2016 - today)

At the centre of it all stands the main central tower, dedicated to Jesus Christ himself.

This tower is the tallest of them all, rising up at 172.5 metres, and has been embellished with a pinnacle of a “four-armed cross”, with added lights radiating in each of the cardinal directions.

Read more about the Jesus Christ tower here.

The Evangelists Towers (2016 - 2023)

Surrounding the main central tower, these 4 towers all stand at 135 metres tall and have been dedicated to each of the Evangelists: Matthew, Mark, John, and Luke.

Each tower can be identified by a unique symbolic entity with wings:

  • - Matthew is represented by an angel.
  • - Mark is represented by a lion.
  • - John is represented by an eagle.
  • - Luke is represented by an ox.

Read more about the Evangelists towers here.

The Nativity Façade (1894 - 1930)

On the east facing side lies the original Nativity façade–the first façade ever constructed for the Basilica de la Sagrada Família.

In accordance with the chronology of construction, this façade represents the birth and life of Jesus in terms of events, with its many Nativity scenes and various decorative elements symbolising nature.

It is divided into 3 distinct portals, each representing a different virtue and member of the Holy Family, and contains 4 bell towers representing the apostles Barnabas, Simon, Jude, and Matthias (when viewing them from left to right).

It is the only façade whose construction began in Antoni Gaudí’s lifetime. Read more about the Nativity façade here.
